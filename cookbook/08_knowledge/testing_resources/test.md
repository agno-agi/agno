# What is a Certified Nurse-Midwife (CNM)?
 Certified Nurse Midwives: 
* Are primary care providers for women throughout the lifespan, with a special emphasis on pregnancy, childbirth, and gynecologic and reproductive health. 
* Come from a background in nursing (RN) and have further obtained advanced graduate-level education in nurse-midwifery (MSN). 
* Provide independent, patient care while practicing collaboratively with provider counterparts, like physicians, anesthesiologists, social workers, registered nurses, and case managers to deliver safe, cost-effective care to healthy women and their families. 
* Have prescriptive authority in all 50 states and must pass a national certification examination and earn national certification as a certified nurse-midwife through the American Midwifery Certification Board (AMCB). 


# How is Midwifery care different than OB care?
Midwifery care involves a trusting relationship between the provider and pregnant person. Our care is individualized, family centered and your pregnancy and birth are viewed as normal life processes. Some highlights resulting from midwifery care include: 

Decreased risk of needing a cesarean.
Higher successful vaginal birth after a past cesarean delivery (VBAC).
Less interventions, with increased use of non-pharmaceutical methods of pain and labor management.
Better outcomes for patients facing racial disparities
Better maternal and neonatal health outcomes both during and after pregnancy.


# Is Midwifery right for me?
Midwifery care is best for women who are healthy (no pre-existing conditions that would require continuous monitoring by an MD), who want to be an active participant in their own care, who are committed to preparing for  both their ideal birth and the unexpected.

# Can you give me more information about doulas, the different types, and the roles they play in my birth?
Oxytocin‚Äôs Role:
Here‚Äôs a perspective on the birth process that may help you decide if a doula feels like a good fit for your birth team. Labor, at its foundation, is a hormonal process. Oxytocin is the hormone that creates contractions which ultimately result in the delivery of a baby. Oxytocin is a shy hormone. It thrives and works most efficiently when you feel relaxed, confident, safe enough that you feel open to being vulnerable. A question to ask yourself: How are we going to create that environment (both inside of you and in the space around you)? Laboring people are extremely emotionally porous and they pick up the energy and dispositions of those around them very quickly so their support people‚Äôs emotions and demeanor are important as well.

Partner Support:
Partners often slip into protector mode, wanting to ensure safety during this intense experience. However, we aim for partners to focus on simply loving and being with you in their unique way. In labor, syncing up, breathing together, and making joint decisions are key for a calm atmosphere fueled by oxytocin. Doulas assist partners in real-time with tools for optimal support, from cultivating a calm demeanor to releasing tension spots. First-time low-risk birthing people might receive guidance to "stay at home as long as possible," even if planning an epidural, which can be stressful and confusing.

At Home:
A birth doula will meet you at your home and help create an environment of calm, peace while helping you and your support team feel confident in the normal processes of labor. Because they have multiple prenatal sessions with you, they will understand your vision and desires of what support looks like for you and when to go to the hospital. 

At Hospital:
Your midwife and nurse team at the hospital will provide physical, emotional, and safety support intermittently. Depending on labor stages, you might see your midwife every few hours and the nurse every 30 mins-2 hours, with solo periods in the labor room. Ask yourself if this enhances relaxation during labor or if continuous doula support is preferable.
Doulas facilitate active participation, fostering a positive birth experience through support, advocacy, and involvement in decision-making. They help you and your support team ask questions, ensuring you advocate for your birth preferences when faced with interventions.

Postpartum Doulas:
Postpartum doulas will help you ease into the transition of new parenthood. They‚Äôll help with troubleshooting issues that may come up, care for your baby when you need a break, do meal prep, assist in helping you nurse and generally be a source of support in your postpartum recovery + help you gain more confidence as a new parent.
